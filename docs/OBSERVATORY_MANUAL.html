01

Reading the dashboard

What each surface tells you at a glance.

The Observatory lays out four working surfaces — a left rail for navigation, a top bar for document-wide controls, a central map workspace, and an inspector panel that expands the currently selected record.

The layout is deliberately flat. There are no modal dialogs for routine reading; instead, panels expand in place so a reviewer can keep both the spatial context of a state and the textual context of its policy record visible at the same time. This chapter walks around the frame before later chapters go under the hood.

1.1 The sidebar

The left rail collapses to a 3.5 rem icon strip at any viewport narrower than a desktop-class window. It holds the analysis entry-point ("New Analysis"), navigation between geography, policy domains, and methodology pages, and the user's workspace profile.

1.2 The top bar

The top bar contains the section switch (Map View, Compare Regions, Policy Timeline, Source Library, Methodology), a search field, a teacher-mode toggle, and notifications. At narrower widths, the less-critical affordances fold away in order: history and notifications first, then teacher-mode, then the export label.

Orientation

A freshly loaded dashboard defaults to Map View with the national aggregate selected. Click any state to open the float inspector on the right; click again to collapse it.

1.3 The inspector

The float inspector is a 22 rem column pinned to the right of the map. It surfaces the selected record's five scored dimensions, its source documents, a confidence readout, and — when present — a teacher-guidance annotation. Chapter 03 unpacks the full record view.

02

The choropleth map

State vs. district modes, colour bands, beacons.

The map is a d3-geo Albers USA projection rendered as inline SVG. Each state is a single path coloured by its policyStrength band; each county is rendered only when the user toggles District view. The map is the primary entry point for every other surface.

2.1 Colour bands

Four colour bands map a numerical score range to a single tile. Queued states — ones that have been scaffolded but not yet scored — fall into a neutral grey.

BandRangeMeaning
High13 – 16Strong formal guidance across most dimensions
Emerging8 – 12Substantive guidance on some dimensions
Minimal1 – 7Light mentions; no formal policy
Uncoded0No extractable evidence yet

2.2 Interactive cues

Three ephemeral cues overlay the map during live playback: a pulse ring on newly updated states, a small "+" beacon when a source has just been ingested, and a thin outline shift when confidence crosses a band threshold.

2.3 District view

Toggling District view swaps the state atlas for us-atlas/counties-10m.json (3 231 counties). District-level policy data is not yet coded — counties are coloured by their parent state record. A banner at the top of the map makes this explicit.

Caveat

Do not read district shading as district-level policy evidence. Two counties in the same state will always show the identical band, regardless of local district practice.

03

Inspecting a state record

Float inspector, source drawer, evidence spans.

Selecting a state opens a record in the inspector. The inspector is the single surface where extraction, evidence, and routing all become visible together; most reviewer workflows begin here.

3.1 Anatomy of a record

A PolicyRecord carries identity metadata, five scored dimensions, derived scores, supporting prose, the list of source documents, and routing outcomes. The inspector arranges these into four stacked bands from top to bottom.

i Identity band
  • State name, abbreviation, year
  • Last updated (ISO 8601)
  • Snapshot status (coded / queued)
When you first open a record, read this band to confirm you are looking at the expected snapshot.
ii Score band
  • Five dimension readouts (0–3 or 0–4)
  • policyStrength out of 16
  • Confidence as a percentage
Chapter 11 defines each dimension; chapter 13 defines confidence.
iii Evidence band
  • Evidence spans grouped by dimension
  • Direct quote + source URL + optional chunk ID
Every non-zero score should surface at least one span here.
iv Routing band
  • Source authority label
  • Approval route + audit status
  • Deep-research flags
Use this band to triage whether a record needs your review.

3.2 The source drawer

Below the score band, the source drawer lists every crawled document attached to the record. Each entry shows its inferred authority level, its publication date guess, and a link out to the original page. Multiple sources of the same level stack in discovery order.

A record can be complete and still unrouted — extraction, evidence, and routing are three separate steps, each of which can fail independently. The inspector surfaces all three so a reviewer can see exactly which stage needs attention.

04

Comparing regions

Multi-state compare view and delta readings.

Compare view accepts up to four state selections and renders their dimension scores side by side. It is the primary surface for cross-jurisdictional questions — does Utah score higher on teacher PD than Washington, and why? — and is where confidence becomes load-bearing.

4.1 What the columns mean

Each selected state becomes a column. Rows are the five scored dimensions plus policyStrength, confidence, source authority, and approval route. Cells are coloured by dimension band, not by absolute rank, so that "minimal" is visibly minimal regardless of which state it belongs to.

4.2 Reading the deltas

The right-most column of each pair shows the delta against a reference state (by default, the first selected). A positive delta means the compared state scores higher on that dimension; a muted delta means the gap is smaller than one confidence band.

A delta smaller than one confidence band should be read as noise, not signal.

This is the single most common reading error: a two-point difference in policyStrength between two low-confidence records is not meaningful. The compare view therefore displays confidence next to every cell.

05

The policy timeline

Snapshot dates, playback, confidence shifts.

The Observatory stores records as year-tagged snapshots rather than a single living document. The timeline view orders those snapshots chronologically and lets the reader scrub through them, watching the national map recolour as policies are issued, amended, or retired.

5.1 Playback mechanics

Playback advances the timeline at a fixed interval. Each tick emits three kinds of visual events: a pulse on states whose policyStrength band changed, a beacon on states that gained a new source document, and an outline shift on states whose confidence crossed 0.75 or 0.90. These thresholds mirror the approval-routing boundaries described in chapter 14.

5.2 Reading a confidence shift

Confidence shifts are the timeline's most informative event. A single-year jump from 0.4 to 0.8 usually indicates a reviewer added evidence spans to previously weakly-scored dimensions, not that the underlying policy changed. The source drawer is the only place to confirm which interpretation applies.

Reading tip

If the map changes colour during playback but no beacon appears, the scoring rubric was revised — not the sources. Check the version history in the colophon of the affected snapshot.

06

Teacher mode

Classroom-oriented framing and guidance surfaces.

Teacher mode re-skins the inspector to emphasise classroom-relevant extracts from the source documents: permitted-use language, assessment disclosure rules, and the prose inside teacherGuidance annotations. Scoring dimensions remain present but visually recede.

6.1 What it changes

The five-dimension readout moves from the top of the inspector to a collapsed drawer. In its place appears a plain-language summary drawn from teacherGuidance.summary (if coded) or, as a fallback, the first two evidence spans attached to aiUseAllowed and assessmentPolicy.

6.2 What it does not change

The underlying data is identical; teacher mode is a rendering, not a filter. A state with no scored teacherGuidance will show the fallback extract rather than a curated summary. The approval route and audit status remain visible in a compact strip at the bottom.

Not for policy decisions

Teacher mode is designed for classroom orientation, not for policy comparison. Do not cite teacher-mode extracts in analytical work — cite the underlying record and its evidence spans directly.

Part Two

Research framework

07

The research design

Agentic policy surveillance: questions, conditions, ablations.

The Observatory is framed as an agentic policy surveillance study rather than a dashboard project. The central claim: a multi-agent, retrieval-grounded pipeline can produce policy surveillance records with limited human intervention while preserving evidence traceability and measurable quality.

This framing has practical consequences for every surface the reader sees. Scores are not opinions; they are outputs of a pipeline that has been experimentally compared against simpler baselines. Confidence is not rhetorical; it is defined by field coverage and evaluated against a gold set. Human review is not a default; it is an escalation path triggered by explicit thresholds.

7.1 Research questions

  1. Can a multi-agent pipeline build policy records with acceptable field-level accuracy?
  2. Does retrieval-grounded verification reduce unsupported coding decisions?
  3. Does a deep-research fallback improve source coverage and citation support?
  4. How much human effort is still required after automated verification and escalation?

7.2 Experimental conditions

Five conditions are compared along a ladder of pipeline complexity. Each adds one component to the previous; ablations (§10.3) remove them one at a time.

7.3 Human-on-the-loop, not in-the-loop

The target operating model separates three human roles. Operations is mostly agentic — humans are not required on every record. Evaluation is mandatory — humans produce gold labels and audit samples. Escalation is conditional — it triggers only when confidence thresholds, source conflicts, or citation gaps fire.

This is human-on-the-loop, not human-in-the-loop. The reviewer is an escalation path, not a bottleneck.
08

System architecture

Agent roles, runtime layers, pipeline flow.

The architecture is structured around a single principle: the app should never read raw crawler output or unchecked LLM output directly. Every field shown in the dashboard has passed through extraction, verification, routing, and publication layers — each of which leaves an audit trail.

8.1 Runtime layers

  • data/generated/raw/*.html
  • data/generated/policy-source-manifest.json
Only layer that touches the open web.
II Evidence layer

III Canonical layer

  • review-queue.json

The only layer the app is permitted to read.

8.2 Agent roles

8.3 Design constraints

  • Every coded field must trace back to one or more evidence spans.
  • Every record must be versioned; updates are additive, not destructive.
  • Parent–child jurisdiction relationships must be explicit.
  • Human reviewers must not be required for every record in routine operation.
09

The analytic coding system

Stages, domains, and auto-approval exclusions.

The coding system formalises how a reviewer should translate a primary document into a PolicyRecord. It is deliberately narrow: six policy-stage values, eight domain labels, three confidence bands, and an explicit list of auto-approval exclusions. The canonical reference is docs/CODEBOOK.md.

9.1 Policy stage

Every record is assigned exactly one policy stage. Stages are ordered by increasing implementation maturity and anchor the implementationStage dimension introduced in chapter 11.

9.2 Policy domains

Up to eight domain labels can be attached to a single record. Labels are assigned only when the source contains explicit policy language or operational guidance for that domain — not when the domain is merely implied.

1 ai_use
  • Permitted use, prohibitions, conditions
Cornerstone domain — almost every record has this.
2 assessment
  • Rules on AI in student evaluation
Overlaps with academic_integrity; tag both when both apply.
3 privacy
  • Student data, vendor posture, FERPA alignment
Requires an explicit privacy clause, not a general policy statement.
4 teacher_pd
  • Training pathways, professional learning
Do not confuse with general PD unrelated to AI.
5 academic_integrity
  • Disclosure, attribution, cheating policy
Often the earliest domain to appear in new policies.
6 procurement
  • Vendor approval, technology purchasing
Rarely present in state-level documents.
7 governance
  • Decision rights, oversight committees
Governance language alone does not imply policy stage.
8 equity
  • Access, language, disability, outcomes
Expect under-counting — equity is often implicit.

9.3 Auto-approval exclusions

Five conditions disqualify a record from auto-approval, regardless of confidence.

Do not auto-approve when

· a coded field has no evidence span
· official sources conflict with one another
· only press coverage is available
· a newer candidate source exists and the current record is stale
· the policy stage depends on inference rather than explicit text

10

Evaluation and ablations

Gold set, metrics, and the ablation design.

Evaluation is the half of the Observatory that makes the other half accountable. The pipeline's outputs are graded against a hand-labelled gold set, broken down by record quality, evidence quality, operational quality, and update quality. A reader who wants to know how good is this? should start here.

10.1 Datasets

DatasetSizePurpose
Development set10 – 20Tune prompts, thresholds, conflict rules
Gold set20 – 50Held-out human labels for evaluation and calibration
Refresh setrollingTest change-detection over time

The gold set carries, per jurisdiction: field labels, evidence spans, source-authority judgement, policy-stage label, and policy-domain labels. It is the reference against which every run is scored.

10.2 Metrics

α Record quality
  • field-level precision
  • field-level recall
  • field-level F1
  • policy-stage accuracy
  • policy-domain accuracy
The headline RQ1 metric is field-level F1.
β Evidence quality
  • citation support rate
  • evidence span precision
  • unsupported claim rate
  • source authority match rate
RQ2: does verification reduce unsupported claims?
γ Operational quality
  • auto-approval rate
  • sample-audit rate
  • human-review rate
  • correction rate after audit
  • mean time to publish
RQ4: how much human effort is still required?
δ Update quality
  • change-detection recall
  • change-detection precision
  • temporal diff accuracy
Applies only to the refresh set.

10.3 Ablations

Four ablations isolate the contribution of each added component. Each removes one layer from Experiment E and re-runs the evaluation on the same gold set.

Ablationi

Remove the verifier

Measures the rise in unsupported-claim rate; quantifies what the verification layer actually catches.

Ablationii

Remove deep research

Measures the drop in source coverage on flagged jurisdictions; quantifies escalation value.

Ablationiii

Remove sampling audit

Measures hidden error rate among mid-confidence auto-publishes.

Ablationiv

Single-agent extraction

Replaces the multi-agent workflow with a single prompt; isolates the benefit of role decomposition.

10.4 Reporting bundle

Every evaluation run emits a bundle containing run metadata, model and prompt versions, source-coverage summary, the four metric families, audit-burden counts, and a top-k list of failure modes. The bundle is the only artefact permitted to claim progress against the baseline.

Part Three

Scoring methodology

11

What is being measured

Five dimensions, score ranges, policy_strength.

The Observatory encodes each state's AI education policy as five small integer scores. The sum of those five scores is the state's policyStrength, bounded on 0–16.

Keeping the rubric this compact is a deliberate trade-off. Fine-grained rubrics are more expressive but impossible to audit at 51-state scale; coarse rubrics are auditable but miss nuance. Five integer-valued dimensions is the point at which a single reviewer can re-score a record in under ten minutes while still producing a usable spread.

11.1 The five dimensions

1 aiUseAllowed
  • Range 0–3
  • Is AI use in classrooms permitted and under what conditions?
3 when integration is explicit, human-centred, and tied to professional learning.
2 assessmentPolicy
  • Range 0–3
  • Rules on AI in assessment and academic integrity
3 when disclosure requirements or standardized assessment positions are stated.
3 privacyPolicy
  • Range 0–3
  • Student-data privacy and vendor posture
3 when the guidance references data-privacy law or vendor-level policy.
4 teacherPdSupport
  • Range 0–3
  • Professional development and training for educators
3 when implementation recommendations or formal educator pathways exist.
5 implementationStage
  • Range 0–4 — note the wider scale
  • Where the policy sits on the life-cycle
4 when the policy is fully operational; 3 when guidance is released; 2 at framework stage.
Σ policyStrength
  • Range 0–16 (sum of the above)
  • The single headline score on the map
Use the breakdown, not the sum, when asking why one state scores higher than another.

11.2 The formula

// scripts/lib/policy-extraction-utils.mjs:33–40
export function computePolicyStrength(record) {
  return (
    (record.ai_use_allowed        ?? 0) +
    (record.assessment_policy     ?? 0) +
    (record.privacy_policy        ?? 0) +
    (record.teacher_pd_support    ?? 0) +
    (record.implementation_stage  ?? 0)
  );
}

11.3 Score labels

ValueLabel
0No coded evidence yet
1Light mention
2Moderate guidance
3Strong formal guidance
4Full operational system
12

How sources are graded

Authority hierarchy and URL classification.

Every source document is assigned an authority level before any scoring happens. Authority is not a judgement of the document's contents — it is a structural claim about where the document came from. A primary statute and a newspaper article about that statute convey the same facts, but only the statute can underwrite an auto-approval.

12.1 How classification works

Levels are inferred from two signals: whether the URL matches an "official" pattern, and whether the title contains level-indicating keywords. A binding-law match must have both — a regex hit on "statute|bill|act|regulation|administrative code|rule" and an official domain.

Official URL definition

// scripts/lib/approval-utils.mjs:33–42
function looksOfficialUrl(url) {
  return (
    url.startsWith("local://") ||
    /\.gov\b/.test(url) ||
    /\.edu\b/.test(url) ||
    /\bwvde\.us\b/.test(url) ||
    /\.state\.[a-z]{2}\.us\b/.test(url) ||
    /\.k12\.[a-z.]+\b/.test(url) ||
    /schools\.[a-z.]+\b/.test(url)
  );
}

12.2 Weak vs. strong sources

Weak
"A state education office has reportedly issued new AI guidance for districts, according to a local news summary."
Strong
"The Department shall adopt guidance for the use of generative AI in instruction and assessment, effective July 1."

The weak example would classify as secondary_reporting; the strong example as binding_law_or_regulation provided the URL sits on a .gov domain.

13

Confidence and evidence

Field coverage, evidence spans, dedupe rules.

Confidence is defined narrowly: it is the proportion of the five scored fields that received a non-zero score. It is not a statistical confidence interval, and it does not reflect the reviewer's subjective certainty.

13.1 The formula

// scripts/extract-policy-records.mjs:117–122
record.confidence = Number(
  (
    [aiUse, assessment, privacy, teacherPd, implementation]
      .filter((field) => field.score > 0).length / 5
  ).toFixed(2)
);

Because the denominator is always five, confidence takes one of six discrete values: 0.0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8, or 1.0. Any record whose confidence appears between these values was produced by a hand-edit to the canonical file and should be spot-checked.

13.2 Evidence spans

Every non-zero score is supposed to be accompanied by at least one evidence span — a direct quote from a source document, attached to the dimension it supports.

interface EvidenceSpan {
  field: string; // which dimension this supports
  quote: string; // verbatim text from the source
  sourceUrl: string; // URL of the document
  chunkId?: string | null;
}

13.3 Deduplication

Evidence spans are deduplicated using the composite key field:quote:source_url. Identical quotes on different URLs are kept; identical quotes on the same URL are not. Per-field, only the three highest-scoring spans survive into the canonical record.

Confidence measures coverage, not certainty. A record can be highly confident and still wrong.
14

Approval routing

Auto-approve, sample audit, human review.

Once a record has been extracted and scored, the routing step decides whether it can be published without further review, whether it should be sampled into an audit queue, or whether it needs a human reviewer to re-read the sources.

14.1 The decision tree

RouteI

auto_approve

All of: confidence ≥ 0.90, authority ∈ {binding_law_or_regulation, official_guidance, official_model_policy}, every scored field has at least one evidence span, no unresolved conflict, authority ∉ forbidden set.

RouteII

sample_audit

All of: 0.75 ≤ confidence ≤ 0.89, at least four of the five scored fields have an evidence span, no unresolved conflict.

RouteIII

human_review

Everything else, including every record with confidence < 0.75, every record flagged by a conflict keyword, and every record whose strongest source is press coverage or secondary reporting.

RouteIV

completed

Terminal state. Assigned once a human reviewer has signed off; the record is exempt from re-routing until its next extraction run.

14.2 Deep-research flags

In parallel with routing, the pipeline flags records for deep research when any of four conditions hold: weak source authority, missing citation on a scored field, source-conflict keywords in the verification notes, or an extraction_status of not_extracted. These flags are advisory — they do not change the route but do appear in the reviewer's triage queue.

Calibration note

The confidence weight dominates deliberately. Changes to the other four signals move the synthesis score by small amounts; changes to field coverage move it sharply. This keeps the synthesis view a proxy for data completeness rather than for editorial strength.

16

Known limitations

Keyword fragility, missing district data, single snapshot.

The Observatory is a working instrument, not a finished artefact. This chapter lists the limits a reader should carry into every use of the dashboard; subsequent editions will close each as the data infrastructure matures.

16.1 Keyword extraction is brittle

The default extractor matches on English keyword co-occurrence. It misses policies written in plain administrative language and over-fires on documents that use its keywords metaphorically. The LLM extraction path (pipeline:gemini) compensates partially but requires independent sampling.

16.2 No academic evidence layer inside the rubric

The rubric grades policies against primary sources only. Although the project is anchored to peer-reviewed work on policy surveillance, legal epidemiology, and structured extraction (see Appendix D), those citations frame the project; they are not attached to individual scores. A future edition may add a references.json layer that binds specific literature to each dimension.

16.3 No sub-state granularity

All records are regionType: "state". The district view is a visual placeholder — counties inherit their parent state's record without modification. Any analysis at district granularity is out of scope until scored district records exist.

16.4 Single-snapshot timeline

The year field exists on every record, but the UI only renders one snapshot at a time. Retrospective comparison across years requires walking the canonical file directly.

16.5 Domain-spoofing edge cases

Source classification relies on URL-domain patterns. Non-standard official domains — territorial governments, tribal education offices, and bilingual press sites — may mis-classify as secondary_reporting. The reviewer checklist in Appendix B catches most of these manually.

A

Pipeline command reference

The eight stages from crawl to publish.

Every published record moves through eight scripted stages. Each stage has an npm entry-point and a well-defined input and output; a reviewer can re-run a single stage without re-running the others.

StageI

Crawl

npm run crawl:sources · seeds → data/generated/raw/*.html + policy-source-manifest.json.

StageII

Scaffold

npm run pipeline:scaffold · manifest → policy-records.json with empty score fields.

StageIII

Chunk

npm run pipeline:chunk · HTML → chunks.jsonl + retrieval-index.json.

StageIV

Extract

npm run pipeline:extract (keyword rules) or pipeline:gemini (LLM) · chunks → scored records.

StageV

Validate

npm run pipeline:validate · schema check against policy-records.schema.json.

StageVI

Route

npm run route-policy-records · records → records with approvalRoute, auditStatus, and deep-research flags.

StageVII

Review

npm run build-review-queue · audit and human-review items routed to reviewers.

StageVIII

Publish

npm run publish-app-data · canonical file → dist/policy-records.json (app-ready).

B

Reviewer checklist

Conditions for auto-approval.

Before a record is auto-approved, every item below must be true. A single failure routes the record to sample_audit or human_review depending on confidence.

checklist:
· at least one source at STRONG_PRIMARY level
· every scored field has ≥ 1 evidence span
· every quote is verbatim from its source
· confidence ≥ 0.90
· verification_notes contains no conflict keyword
· review_status ≠ "needs_revision"
· sourceAuthority ∉ {press_release, secondary_reporting}

If any box is unchecked, record the reason in routingReasons and return the record to the queue. The reviewer checklist is designed to be re-runnable: a re-extract that fixes one reason can clear the record without re-reviewing the others.

D

References & informing literatures

Primary sources, internal methodology docs, and the literatures this project draws from.

A note on what this appendix is, and what it is not. The Observatory does not cite academic papers inside its scoring pipeline — every numerical value in a record is grounded in a primary policy document, not in the pedagogy or privacy literature. The project is, however, anchored to a compact set of peer-reviewed work on policy surveillance, legal epidemiology, AI-in-education governance, and structured information extraction; those citations frame the project's design and are the same list surfaced on the public project-overview page. This appendix therefore does four things: it names the primary source authorities the rubric trusts, it enumerates the internal methodology documents that govern the project, it lists the cited literatures that anchor the project's framing, and it calls out the gaps where citations are still missing.

D.1 Primary source authorities

All 479 crawled seeds (see data/source-seeds.json) resolve to one of the following categories. Each individual URL functions as a reference in its own right; the full list is machine-readable in the seed file.

  • U.S. state departments of education (51 jurisdictions, including DC)
  • State boards of education and standards bodies
  • State statutes, administrative codes, and legislative bill pages
  • State-issued model policies and implementation frameworks
  • Agency press releases that point to a primary source

D.3 Cited literatures — and the pragmatic adaptation

The six works below appear on the public project-overview page under Academic Grounding (see src/components/ProjectOverviewPage.tsx). They were chosen because each supplies a concrete design principle a solo-researcher, code-first project could realistically adopt. §D.4 documents the pragmatic adaptation — what was borrowed, what was simplified, and what was deliberately deferred. The goal is working research infrastructure on a short horizon, not a faithful reproduction of any single paper's full methodology.

  1. Kavanagh, M. M., Meier, B. M., Pillinger, M., Huffstetler, H., & Burris, S. (2020). Global policy surveillance: Creating and using comparative national data on health law and policy. American Journal of Public Health, 110(12), 1805–1810.

    borrowed The idea that scattered policy instruments should be converted into a single structured, comparable table of jurisdictions. The Observatory operationalises this as the canonical record (src/types.ts, data/canonical/policy-records.schema.json) with a fixed rubric shared across all states. A year field is built in so a future edition can stack snapshots without schema churn.

  2. Poirier, M. J. P., Viens, A. M., Penney, T. L., Van Katwyk, S. R., Astbury, C. C., Lin, G., Nanyangwe-Moyo, T., & Hoffman, S. J. (2022). Principles and methods of global legal epidemiology. Journal of Epidemiology & Community Health, 76(9), 828–832.

    borrowed The commitment to repeatable coding procedures. Instead of running a full inter-rater reliability study, the project's pragmatic substitute is a fixed prompt-level rubric (scripts/extract-policy-records-gemini.mjs), a five-level source-authority hierarchy (§12), and a gold set that replays as a regression test. It is a compressed version of the method, not a replacement for κ.

  3. Ramanathan, T., Hulkower, R., Holbrook, J., & Penn, M. (2017). Legal epidemiology: The science of law. Journal of Law, Medicine & Ethics, 45(S1), 69–72.

    borrowed The stance that policy is a measurable object worth coding, not a narrative to summarise. The Observatory takes this as licence to reduce policy language to rubric integers (§11) so jurisdictions become comparable. Linking those codes to downstream outcomes is out of scope for this edition — the paper's framing is used to justify measurement, not to claim an effect study.

  4. Ifenthaler, D., Majumdar, R., Gorissen, P., Judge, M., Mishra, S., Raffaghelli, J., & Shimada, A. (2024). Artificial intelligence in education: Implications for policymakers, researchers, and practitioners. Technology, Knowledge and Learning, 29, 1693–1710.

    borrowed The domain list that the paper says AI-in-education policy must cover — AI use, assessment, privacy, teacher support, implementation readiness. These are the five scored dimensions in §11 and the five required fields in the canonical schema. Academic integrity, procurement, and governance appear as additional domain tags so the coding surface is broader than the scored core.

  5. Dagdelen, J., Dunn, A., Lee, S., Walker, N., Rosen, A. S., Ceder, G., Persson, K. A., & Jain, A. (2024). Structured information extraction from scientific text with large language models. Nature Communications, 15, 1418.

    borrowed The method — schema-constrained LLM extraction with field-level evidence. Implemented directly: scripts/extract-policy-records-gemini.mjs passes a responseJsonSchema to Gemini with responseMimeType: "application/json", and every rubric field is required to return an evidence span (field / quote / source_url / chunk_id). This is the one citation the repository implements at close to the level the paper describes.

  6. C R, C., Kulkarni, S., Sagi, S. R. A. V., Pandey, S., Yalavarthy, R., Chakraborty, D., & Upadhyay, P. D. (2024). LeGen: Complex information extraction from legal sentences using generative models. In Proceedings of the Natural Legal Language Processing Workshop 2024 (pp. 1–17).

    borrowed The argument that legal/policy text demands specialised extraction rather than keyword matching. The pragmatic response is to run retrieval over chunked policy text and bind each rubric score to a quoted span — evidence-grounded extraction rather than LeGen's clause-level relations. The paper motivates why the keyword extractor alone is not enough (§16.1), not the specific relation-extraction architecture.

D.4 Pragmatic adaptation in the code

Each paper was adopted with a single, explicit simplification that a solo-researcher project could ship. The table below names what was borrowed, what the adaptation looks like in the code, and the shortcut that was taken to stay buildable.

Citation What was borrowed How it shows up in the code Pragmatic shortcut
Kavanagh et al. (2020) Structure scattered policies into a single comparable table keyed by jurisdiction. Canonical record schema (src/types.ts, data/canonical/policy-records.schema.json), state-level records rendered uniformly in the dashboard. Single-snapshot per state; the year field is provisioned for future longitudinal use but not yet populated (§16.4).
Poirier et al. (2022) Repeatable coding procedures rather than ad-hoc judgement. Prompt-level rubric with fixed score bands, source-authority hierarchy (§12), and a replayable gold set used as regression fixtures. A compressed stand-in for a full inter-rater study — no computed κ or dual coding, just prompt and gold-set discipline.
Ramanathan et al. (2017) Treat policy as something measurable, not only narrative. Every rubric dimension reduces to an integer band, enabling aggregation and cross-state comparison (§11). The project measures policy content, not policy effects — linking codes to outcomes is out of scope.
Ifenthaler et al. (2024) The governance-relevant domain list AI-in-education policy has to cover. Five scored rubric dimensions (AI use, assessment, privacy, teacher PD, implementation) map 1:1 to the paper's emphasis; policy_domains enum carries the wider surface. Oversight/accountability surfaces as governance, auto-derived from implementation_stage ≥ 3 (scripts/lib/policy-extraction-utils.mjs) rather than coded directly.
Dagdelen et al. (2024) Schema-constrained LLM extraction with evidence. scripts/extract-policy-records-gemini.mjs uses responseJsonSchema + responseMimeType; every rubric field returns a required evidence span; canonical records carry extraction_model and coder_type: "hybrid". No meaningful shortcut — this is the method, applied.
C R et al. (2024, LeGen) Specialised policy/legal-text extraction rather than keyword heuristics. Retrieval over chunked policy text (pickTopChunksByField) feeding Gemini extraction; keyword path retained only as fallback (§16.1). Evidence-grounded rubric extraction stands in for clause-level relation extraction — LeGen is used as motivation, not architecture.

D.5 Coverage map of the cited literature

The four framings below describe what the citation set is asked to carry. Read alongside §D.4: the map also flags where named prior work is still missing.

i Policy surveillance
  • Comparable jurisdiction records
  • Standardised coding, reproducibility
  • Temporal policy tracking
Cited: Kavanagh et al. (2020); Poirier et al. (2022); Ramanathan et al. (2017).
Deferred: longitudinal snapshots, formal inter-rater reliability, policy-as-intervention outcome linkage.
ii AI-in-education governance
  • Assessment, privacy, teacher support
  • Oversight and accountability
  • Domain-level coding targets
Cited: Ifenthaler et al. (2024).
Deferred: oversight/accountability as a separately coded dimension rather than a maturity-derived proxy.
iii Structured / legal IE
  • Schema-constrained LLM extraction
  • Evidence-span grounding
  • Legal-text specialisation
Cited: Dagdelen et al. (2024) — implemented; C R et al. (2024) — used as motivation.
Deferred: clause-level relation extraction and rhetorical-role tagging.
iv Multi-agent & human-on-the-loop
  • Role decomposition (scout, extractor, verifier)
  • Confidence-triggered escalation
  • Selective audit sampling
Not cited. Shapes §8.2 agent roles and §14 routing logic but currently rests on engineering practice rather than named prior work.
Pragmatic stance

The six citations are adopted as design principles, not reproduced as full methodologies. Dagdelen is implemented at close to the paper's level; the three legal-epidemiology works (Kavanagh, Poirier, Ramanathan) are compressed into one working codebook-plus-rubric; Ifenthaler supplies the domain list; LeGen motivates evidence-grounded extraction without its clause-level apparatus. The deferred items in §D.4's shortcut column are the honest to-do list for a next edition — longitudinal snapshots, computed reliability, separately coded oversight, and a named citation for the multi-agent layer.
